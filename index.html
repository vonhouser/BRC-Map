Boats, Buoys, Kayaks, Launches. Know How to Share the Waterways.

The traffic patterns are designed so that all the boats in one path are going in the same direction. Everyone should be familiar with them, and coaches, coxes, bow persons and singles rowers must know and adhere to them completely. Use common sense. When crossing traffic, look both ways. Make any turns far enough from obstacles (like the bridge) so that you can be seen by other boats. Pull off the traffic pattern if you need to stop, donâ€™t make sudden turns in front of other boats. Never assume that power boats see you, and stay away from them. Be ready to yield the right-of-way. The Coast Guard regulations say that nothing exonerates a boat, its crew or master if they neglect to maintain a proper look-out, go too fast, fail to take action to avoid collision or ignore their responsibilities. Note that the crossing area coming out from the dock and the area around the double day/green marker and the red channel marker at Ferry Bar are high traffic areas. Be extra observant when approaching these areas.

Day markers. They Are Your Friend.Know Where They Are and Where They Should Be.

A daymark or a day marker is a structure, in our case either a red triangle or a green square that serves as an aid to navigation by boaters. Similar in concept to a lighthouse, a daymark does not have a light and so is usually only visible during daylight hours, so if you are rowing at night, be very careful. These markers are key to understanding the traffic pattern and where you should be on the water.

The real reason we obey the traffic pattern. Just Do It.

If you violate the traffic pattern, you could injure yourself, others, or the equipment. The goal of the traffic pattern is to keep everyone safe, just like on the roads. Especially since we row in the dark! But ultimately, we also would like to avoid getting this look from Head Coach Marc Daemen.
